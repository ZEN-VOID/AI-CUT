# 王建硕 as a Writer: The Spontaneous Side

Research file 02 — how Wang Jian Shuo writes about his own writing, interacts with
readers and commenters, explains things, admits error, and what he holds back.

**Source note:** All quotes below are first-hand source material — Wang's own blog
posts (wangjianshuo.com, 2002–2014), reproduced verbatim from the parsed corpus
including his original typos and grammar. Every claim is cited by post date + title.
"Stated" = he says it explicitly. "Inferred" = pattern observed across posts.
Contradictions are preserved, not resolved.

---

## 1. How he describes his own writing process

### Why he writes — stated reasons, and they multiply over time

He is unusually transparent that the *origin* was almost trivial and that he
back-rationalized a mission later.

- **The "post my resume" origin (half-joke, but he means it).**
  > "Well. I just want to build a website, so I can post my resume one day."
  > … "I was kidding. But it was one of the reasons."
  > — *2004-07-01, I Started the Blog to Post Resume*. He ties it to the parable of
  > two monks: one digs a well daily, one doesn't. "It reveals the importance of
  > accumulation. I thought to write something everyday may be a good idea."

- **The "article a day, any topic" origin.** Stated repeatedly that Shanghai content
  was not the plan — it *accreted*.
  > "I remember the initial thoughts was just to write one article per day, no matter
  > what topic I write. … there is no Shanghai information at the very begining. Later
  > … I found by sharing my personal life, I am sharing about what is happening in
  > this city, and then the blog became THE Shanghai blog."
  > — *2007-07-25, Write Articles, Not Blog Postings*

- **The mission ("personal guide to Shanghai") is a later overlay.** By 2006 he
  states it cleanly: "I want to be visitor's personal guide to this amazing city. I
  try to help people to live better in Shanghai." — *2006-04-22, Welcome to
  Wangjianshuo's Blog*. But he keeps flagging it is downstream of the daily habit.

- **Blogging as discipline / meditation, and as proof of self-management.**
  > "Blogging is the time to force me think and keep observation. … The strongest
  > reason I convince myself to continue is, 'If I cannot manage to get some time
  > writing, how can I manage a business bigger than that?'"
  > — *(2005 New Year reflection, cited in corpus line 39966)*

### When he writes — late at night, and he tells you so

This is stated openly and used to pre-excuse quality lapses.

- > "Typically the blogs were written at late night." — *2006-09-11, Four Years of Blogging*
- > "most of my entries were created around mid-night, the time my IQ for language is
  > among the lowest. I guess fifteen minutes more sleep is more appreciated for a
  > sleepyhead like me in mid-night." — *2005-09-20, Not Perfect English is Fine, So does Correction*
- He often signs off mid-thought: "Have a wonderful night - sleepyhead Jian Shuo is
  going to bed now." (same post). The 23:00–24:00 writing window is mentioned as
  early as *2003-04-21*: "spent quite some time every night - from 23:00 to 24:00 -
  to keep this site up to date."

### Why daily — faithfulness to readers

He adopted this as a *rule*, lifted from Mark Bernstein's "10 Tips on Writing the
Living Web," which he calls "the guideline for my blog writing."
> "If you don't write for a few days, you are unfaithful to the readers who come to
> visit. Missing an update is a small thing — rudeness, not betrayal."
> — quoted approvingly in *2004-01-14, Write Better Weblog*

He literally created a post just to admit he had nothing: *2006-04-06, What to Write
Today?* — full text is essentially "Suddenly found there is no topic to write today.
… So, what should I write today?" Inferred: the daily cadence outranks having
something to say.

### Attitude toward his non-native English and typos

This is one of his clearest, most consistent self-positions — **imperfection is
accepted, openly, as the price of the daily habit; correction is welcomed; but he
will not be ashamed.**

- **Stated awareness, no excuses-as-evasion:**
  > "But English is not my native language and there are still many grammar and
  > spelling errors in every article. I am aware of it." — *2004-06-16, Volunteers Wanted*

- **The fullest statement of his stance** — *2005-09-20, Not Perfect English is Fine,
  So does Correction*:
  > "I admit I felt a little bit embarrassed whenever someone pointed out some errors
  > on my blog. I felt even stronger when the erros are so obvious. I tried to correct
  > the original version if the suggestion makes sense. Don't worry. I am not
  > discouraged at all for these cases. I think I have built enough confidence after
  > the three years of blogging. **Being confident means to truly understand what one
  > can do and what one cannot, and to accept imperfectness.**"

- He thanks correctors by name and treats correction as collaboration:
  > "I write this article to thank John, from Sinosplice, to send me emails to correct
  > typos and grammar errors in my previous entry." — *(corpus line 19784)*
  And he recruited volunteer "Proof Reading Experts" via Wiki — *2004-06-16,
  Volunteers Wanted*: "If you enjoy reading this blog and cannot bear the bad grammar
  of any entry, why not help me?"

- He even ran a deliberate typo experiment on his own name:
  > "I changed my display name from 'Jian Shuo Wang @ Beijing' to 'Jain Suho Wnag @
  > Bieijng' three days ago … It was an experiment and it was just for fun."
  > — *2004-08-09, Jain Suho Wnag @ Bieijng* (he then quotes the famous
  > scrambled-letters paragraph). Inferred: imperfect spelling doesn't threaten him —
  > he plays with it.

- **Contradiction worth preserving:** he *also* says basic spell-checking matters and
  feels guilty when it slips — "keeping the most basic spelling and grammar checking
  is also critical for a good blog … let me give blogging more time to correct those
  obvious grammar and spelling errors." (*2005-09-20*) So: accepts imperfection in
  principle, still nags himself about it in practice.

### "Articles, not blog postings" — his quality ideal

> "To treat every entry on the blog as an standalone article that is valuable even
> after years is a very good way to think of blog." … He worries Google AdSense
> traffic-chasing corrupts this: "I feel I am losing the faith to create an article
> (I do mean A-R-T-I-C-L-E), and sometimes started to create blogs."
> — *2007-07-25, Write Articles, Not Blog Postings*

### The soul of a blog is content, not tech (despite his constant tinkering)

> "Am I working too much on the technical side of this site? I often surpress my
> attempt to drive into code although I enjoy writing some small piece of codes. As I
> said, the soul of a blog is the content, not the technology it is using."
> — *2004-09-16, Search Function Improved*. (Contradiction preserved: a large share
> of his posts are in fact about MovableType, hosting, CGI scripts, RSS.)

## 2. Reader interaction patterns

### How he addresses readers — warm, plural, gratitude-first

- Default register is grateful and inclusive. He routinely opens or closes with
  thanks: "I want to thank everyone for commenting on my blog." (*2003-04-03, Thanks
  for your Comments*); "Thanks every one for being with me." (*2006-09-11*).
- He ran monthly **"Top Commenter"** posts for years (2003–2007), listing readers by
  name and comment count — treating commenters as named contributors, not an
  audience. > "the page is created by a great group of people - the commentors."
  (*2008-04-12, What the Term China … Means?*)
- He repeatedly **turns decisions over to readers**: > "Whenever I meet any tough
  decision related to this site, I turn to my readers to help me." — *2004-08-10,
  Shall I Open a Shanghai BBS*. Also created *Suggest a Topic* (2006-10-24) and
  *Where are You?* (2006-10-21) as standing reader-input pages.
- He genuinely *reads back* what readers tell him about themselves and lets it change
  his curiosity: > "now if someone talks about Holland … now I get excited and say:
  'Hey! I know someone in Holland!'" — *2006-10-21, Where are You?*

### His stated "principle on comments" — consistent across years

First articulated *2003-04-03, Thanks for your Comments*, restated almost verbatim
*2006-10-04, Remove Comments? Maybe Not*:
> "my principle on comments is not [to] delete any comment as long as it is legal,
> not spam and is readable, and (this one is important) it is NOT personal attack."
- He will **not edit** a comment: "I don't change the content of any comment although
  the system provide the function. If I disagree, I will post my comment under it."
  (*2003-04-03*)
- The **one line he enforces** is protecting *other readers*: "I cannot bear anyone
  writing flaming comment against my other readers." (*2003-04-03*) He hid comments
  that attacked a commenter named "bob," explaining: "Thank you for your defense for
  me, but … I value any disagreement as much as those supporting comments. I don't
  think (helping me by flaming other commenters) hurts me as much as people's attempt
  to discourage others from express their opinion." — *2005-09-20*

### How he handles disagreement and correction

- **Disagreement is welcomed, even sought.** From *Write Better Weblog* (2004-01-14)
  he adopts "Find good enemies" as a rule: "I didn't under this rule at the very
  beginning, but later, I found an enemy is good to better clarify the nature of the
  idea." Quoting Bernstein: "Disagreement is exciting."
- **He responds to disagreement publicly, in-thread or in a new post**, rather than
  privately — and is self-aware that this is a blogger tic: > "In a typical blogger
  manner, I response publicly (nothing identify a blogger better than talking
  publicly on blogs when the mobile phone … is easily available)." — *2014-10-24,
  Blog is Dead - Let's Move On*
- When a reader (mac) was agitated, his reply is patient and de-escalating: > "@mac,
  calm down. I like discussion, although I don't expect myself to answer every single
  comment, and even if I try to join some discussion, I will try to carefully state
  what I believe, with no expectation that the other end may agree with me or even
  understand." — *2008-04-13, Not Just Identify Problems for China - Solve Them!*

### How he treats being misunderstood — this is high-emotion territory

Unlike the calm correction stance, being *misunderstood* visibly hurts him. The
key text is *2003-04-21, How do you Feel if Being Misunderstood?* — a spammer-related
commenter accused him of running his anti-spam forum as covert self-advertising:
> "I am very angry when I saw his comment."
> "the comments about the evil motivation really let me feel very upset."
> "it is very discouraging to see comments like this. I threw money to my ISP … and
> spent quite some time every night … to keep this site up to date. … But how do you
> feel if you are misunderstood? How do YOU feel?"
His move when misunderstood: **lay out the full factual timeline** ("It is the simple
7 steps") and **appeal to readers for support** ("I need your support"). He
distinguishes careless misreaders he forgives ("they were just careless and didn't
mean to hurt me") from someone who "obversely checked out the site" and still
attacked — only the latter angers him.

Note the title of an early apology comment: he signs it "**Jian Shuo Wang, The
Copier**" (*2002-09, andersja CSS dispute*) — even while defending himself he adopts
the accuser's label with a kind of rueful humility.

### Boundaries he set on interaction (the openness retreats over time)

There is a clear **arc from radical openness to managed limits** as traffic grew:
- He posted his **mobile number** and *loved* the calls: "I Love to Receive Calls …
  every call I received was a pleasant story." — *2004-09-16, Posting my Mobile Number*
- But by 2005 he **removed his email** from the site: > "It is very bad that someone
  put effort to write an email to me, waitted somewhere in the world, but I don't
  have the ability to reply. … It is not easy to balance being 'open' … or being
  realistic … I finally choose the easy one." — *2005-09-19, Removed my Email from Blog*
- Recurring guilt about email backlog: "If you wrote to me in the last week, chances
  are, you will NOT get a response. Send again if you do care a response. Sorry for
  that, but my bandwidth is limited." — *2005-03-20, My Blog Won't Go Commercial*.
  Also *2004-12-18*: "Excuse me for not replying comments … sorry for conciseness of
  the reply."
- Later still: *2007-07-14, Please Don't Add me on MSN Messenger* (title alone shows
  the boundary). Inferred pattern: he keeps apologizing for limits rather than
  asserting them coldly — the warmth stays even as the door narrows.

## 3. His Q&A / explanatory mode

Many posts answer a specific reader question. The structure is remarkably consistent.

### Structure of an explanation

Typical shape (e.g. *2006-10-23, China Eastern Airlines*; *2007-01-10, 120K
Self-Claim Tax in Details*; *2006-10-22, Cash on Delivery Still Preferred*):
1. **Quote the reader's actual question verbatim**, often as a block.
2. **State the source/limit of his own knowledge** before answering.
3. **Answer in labeled mini-sections** (he loves bolded sub-heads: "Booking /
   Payment / e-Ticket", "Who Should Follow / How to Claim it / What is the Status?").
4. **Generalize one level up** at the end — turn the specific answer into a small
   observation about how China/Shanghai/the world works.

He explicitly values the question itself: > "To ask the right question is more
important than answering it, isn't it." — *2006-10-23, China Eastern Airlines*

### Patience level — high, and he frames Q&A as mutually useful

He doesn't treat questions as a burden on the *content* side (only on the *volume*
side). Questions are his topic-generation engine:
> "The questions helped me to find good topics to write about Shanghai." — *2005-09-19*
> "if you have any specific questions about what the real situation is, feel free to
> ask me, and the questions help me to observe what is happening around me better."
> — *2009-02-19, Financial Crisis and Shanghai*

### Assumed-knowledge level — deliberately low, deliberately non-expert

This is a *stated, defended* editorial choice:
- > "I am not an expert in daily life in Shanghai. … What I did was just to share the
  > little knowledge I know about the city … Just because I was not the expert, many
  > practices are more useful since most of people have the taste of a non-expert."
  > — *2006-02-17, A Blog is All About a Person*
- > "I especially put the first time visitors in mind, so some introduction may seem
  > obvious, but it is NOT for first time visitors." — *2006-09-11, Four Years of Blogging*
- He explains culturally-assumed things to outsiders carefully — e.g. why Spring
  Festival train tickets are scarce: "I am not sure if people outside China
  understand this, people will try to return home no matter where they are in the
  Spring Festival, just like Christmas in the western countries." — *2003-01-19,
  Tickets for Maglev Tour*

### The recurring disclaimer habit

He compulsively marks the epistemic status of his explanations:
- > "Disclaimer: Just like previous post, this is just what I *THINK* it works. It is
  > NOT an official explain of the tax issue." — *2007-01-10, 120K Self-Claim Tax*
- "(correct me if I am wrong here)" appears dozens of times across the corpus,
  attached to facts about coins, bike taxes, bus fares, the U.S. Constitution, free
  trade zones, etc. Representative: > "after hours of study of the U.S. Consitutation
  … Correct me if I am wrong, since to pretend I know U.S. Constitution, or I am a
  researcher in this field is deadly wrong." — *(corpus line 28124)*

## 4. Moments he changes his mind or admits error (high-signal — quoted)

He admits error often, plainly, sometimes with intensifiers. A spectrum:

- **Small factual self-correction, cheerful:**
  > "Well. I admit that I made a mistake here. It is not a beach. It is just a lake
  > with some sand on the north side of it." — *(corpus line 39808)*

- **Technical hubris punctured, immediately:**
  > "I was wrong, deadly wrong. Within one minute, a new comment spam appeared, using
  > the new comment script." — *(corpus line 1778, 2006)*

- **A whole worldview-level admission** — his initial reaction to the 2008 Sichuan
  earthquake:
  > "Even when I know it was in the mountain area in Sichuan, I said: 'It is lucky
  > that it is in very rural area, and not many people are living there'. I was wrong,
  > deadly wrong." — *2008-05, (corpus line 29924)*

- **Admitting a conceptual mistake and writing a follow-up post to fix it** — after
  his Olympic-boycott post he realized he'd conflated "China the government" with
  "China the people":
  > "I admit, during my last post on boycott, I made a mistake … to be confused about
  > the term of a country name. … I was sometimes mistakenly mix all these concept
  > together. We need to be very careful about it in the future." — *2008-04-12, What
  > the Term China, or France Means?*

- **Admitting he was wrong about the value of his own work** (changed mind in the
  optimistic direction):
  > "Recently, I found I was wrong. If a program can impact even one person, it makes
  > difference. It is not quantitatively significant, but qualitatively significant."
  > — *(corpus line 6539 / 20293)*

- **A losing-bet admission**, with self-deprecation:
  > "I whispered to Wendy: 'I don't think there is anyone who should guess more than
  > 66.67.' … It turned out that I am wrong, since many people choose that one."
  > — *(corpus line 30260)*

- **Behavioral self-honesty** (not factual error, but admitting weakness):
  > "I made a mistake. I should not have opened Nexus One at night. I just cannot
  > resist the temptation to open it … I spent about the last one hour … to play with
  > it." — *(corpus line 2365, 2010)*

- **The andersja CSS dispute (2002)** — his earliest big public reckoning. He admits
  fault four separate times in one comment ("Firstly, I apologize"; "Anyway, I
  apologize"; "I admit I did it"; "Sometimes I may make mistakes and I will be fully
  responsible for that") while *also* defending the facts ("But the true is, I did
  link back"). And explicitly: "Please be assured that I am not 'fighting back' in
  this comment." Inferred template: **apologize fully + present facts + invite
  further correction**, all at once, without treating apology and self-defense as
  mutually exclusive.

Stated meta-position on being right: > "As always, I don't want to pretend that I am
always right. … This is just my perspective (everyone has an angle, right?)"
— *2008-04-11 boycott post (corpus line ~28491)*

## 5. What he refuses to write about / where he holds back

### Politics — engages, but carefully and with hedging (this shifts over time)

- **Early stance: avoid "big terms."** He repeatedly shrinks his own scope on
  purpose: > "It may be too late for me to write about the financial crisis … (I
  tried hard to avoid to use too big terms like China - basically, I only know a very
  small portion of the city of Shanghai, not to mention about China)." — *2009-02-19,
  Financial Crisis and Shanghai*. Same move in *2009-05-17*: "I tried to avoid big
  topics like Education in China."
- **But he does NOT refuse politically sensitive topics outright** — and over
  2008–2009 he engages directly: the Tibet/Torch posts, and notably Xu Zhiyong's
  detention: > "I will closely monitor what happens to Zhiyong, and provide my help
  if needed (including update the status of the case on this blog)." — *2009-07-31,
  Welcome Home, Chris and Endeavour*. He calls the Great Firewall "certainly a shame
  in our country" (*2008-04-13*).
- His self-imposed rule for politics is **method, not silence**: state it as a normal
  person's perspective, invite the other side to educate him, focus on *solutions*.
  > "I need to do something to HELP SOLVING the problem, instead of doing something
  > just because everyone is doing." — *2008-04-13*
- **Contradiction / arc to preserve:** the English-language corpus thins out
  dramatically after ~2009 (corpus_2015–2022 files are tiny). *2013-05-26, I am Back*
  obliquely references "uneasy three years … the unstable mood of myself" without
  detail — a visible *holding back* on personal/company turmoil. So: he held back
  less on macro-politics than expected, but held back more on his own bad periods.

### Privacy — a stated, principled boundary, mostly about *other people*

- His rule is explicit: > "Protect other's privacy. … People can choose whatever
  he/she want to write about his/her own life, but don't involve other people's life.
  The rule of thumb I use is, ask for permission if you reveals the information for
  other persons. To tell people what you eat for last dinner is no problem … but to
  share what the other person ate last night seems violating this person's privacy."
  — *2006-02-12, Privacy of Blogging*
- He applies it to himself: an email from astronaut Chris Cassidy from the ISS — > "that
  very exciting and interesting email is private communication and should not be
  published, otherwise, I would be very happy to share." — *2009-07-31*
- About his *own* privacy he is relaxed (posted his mobile number, address-level
  detail), but: "Blogging is NOT a private diary." — *2006-02-12*. He holds back the
  diary-confessional register, not facts about himself.

### Company specifics — present but bounded

He writes about Baixing as a CEO ("we tried very hard to build a great company" —
*2012-02-03, What is CEO's Job*) and posts a friend's job listing once while warning
"I generally don't do this" (*2013-01-25*). Inferred: he'll share management
*philosophy* freely but rarely operational/confidential company detail.

### Commercialization — a hard, stated refusal

> "I am not surprised if people ask me … 'Why did you turn your blog into a
> commercial site? I am sure you can make a lot if you sell it'. … But the problem
> is, I won't sell it. … It is part of my life. … The worst thing I can do is to get
> money from someone … and I lose the passion to stay late and write. I may mention
> something in a dishonest way just because I get money from it. 1% presence of such
> dishonest content ruins my confident to write." — *2005-03-20, My Blog Won't Go
> Commercial*. He also politely refused reader *donations*: "I want to respectfully
> reject it. … If you want to donate, I accept gifts - a post card with international
> stamp is a good choice, and a link to this site is another good one."

## 6. Tone toward strangers / the world

**Dominant register: warm, curious, trusting, gratitude-forward — defensive only
when motive is attacked.**

- **Trust toward strangers is a defining trait.** Posting his mobile number, his
  address-level detail, hosting other people's blogs for free, opening his city map
  to anonymous Wiki edits — all framed as obviously worth it. On the map: "Then I
  opened the right to add a point to anyone who has access to the page. This is built
  upon the Wiki concept." — *2004-10-17, My Map Has Errors - I Know*
- **Curiosity about every stranger's country.** *Where are You?* (2006-10-21) exists
  purely so readers will tell him about Finland, the Philippines, Denmark — and he
  follows up reading their histories.
- **He assumes good faith first.** Careless commenters: "they were just careless and
  didn't mean to hurt me." (*2003-04-21*) Even the spam-abuse forwarder he calls
  "Beautiful fight back, SImo." (*Net Send Spam post, 2003*)
- **Defensiveness is narrow and specific:** it triggers on *imputed bad motive*
  ("evil motivation," being called a fraud, being told his help "is DEFINATELY NOT to
  help you"), not on factual disagreement or grammar correction. Against motive
  attacks he gets visibly angry and goes to a fact-by-fact defense + appeal to the
  community.
- **Self-deprecation is constant and disarming** — "sleepyhead Jian Shuo," "The
  Copier," "I am the dummy who don't understand the brand" (*2010-10-10*), "a
  confused speculator in this strange place" (*2010-10-10*), "I am still confused, but
  …" (*2002-11-14*). He almost never claims authority; he claims to be a "normal
  person" whose ordinariness is the value proposition.
- **Closing warmth as a verbal tic:** "Thank you for staying with me." (*2006-04-22*);
  "Life is always beautiful, so record it well." (*2006-09-11*); "For me, every
  single day is a holiday." (*2006-04-22*).

**One preserved tension:** the warmth is real but the *bandwidth* is not infinite —
across a decade you can watch him repeatedly apologize for not being able to keep up
with the openness he set in motion (email removed, MSN closed, comments unreplied).
The tone never turns cold; it turns *guilty*. He would rather apologize for a
boundary than state it without apology.
